## Pitching is three problems

<div class="grid-3" style="margin-top: 1.2em;">
<div class="card card-accent">

</div>
<div class="card card-rose">

</div>
<div class="card card-indigo">

<p style="font-size: 0.88em;">How you get that narrative + deck in front of the people who matter.</p>

## A pitch is not a briefing.

<p class="mt-1" style="color: #999;">🚫 Summary &nbsp;&nbsp; 🚫 Feature list &nbsp;&nbsp; 🚫 Product explainer</p>
<p style="margin-top: 0.7em; color: #e5e5e5;"><strong><em>Pitches are narratives that inspire people to action.</em></strong></p>

Notes:
First thing to internalize. If your pitch sounds like a status update: here's what we did, here's what we learned, here's what we're doing next. You've written a briefing, not a pitch.

## A framework for narratives

<div style="margin-top: 1em;">
<div class="card card-accent">
<h4>Andy Raskin's Strategic Narrative</h4>
<p style="font-size: 0.9em;">Five elements. Used by Zuora, Drift, Salesforce, and hundreds of others. Battle-tested on the world's hardest audiences.</p>

## Raskin's five elements

<div style="display: flex; flex-direction: column; gap: 0.45em; margin-top: 0.8em;">
  <div style="display: grid; grid-template-columns: 2.2em 1fr; gap: 0.7em; align-items: center; padding: 0.5em 0.9em; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 4px solid #f97316; background: #fff7f0;">
    <span style="font-weight: 800; font-size: 1.1em; color: #f97316; text-align: center;">1</span>
    <div><strong>Name a big change in the world</strong> <span style="color: #777; font-size: 0.88em;">your problem, reframed as a shift</span></div>
  </div>
  <div style="display: grid; grid-template-columns: 2.2em 1fr; gap: 0.7em; align-items: center; padding: 0.5em 0.9em; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 4px solid #e11d48; background: #fff1f2;">
    <span style="font-weight: 800; font-size: 1.1em; color: #e11d48; text-align: center;">2</span>
    <div><strong>Show there'll be winners and losers</strong> <span style="color: #777; font-size: 0.88em;">stakes</span></div>
  </div>
  <div style="display: grid; grid-template-columns: 2.2em 1fr; gap: 0.7em; align-items: center; padding: 0.5em 0.9em; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 4px solid #059669; background: #f0fdf4;">
    <span style="font-weight: 800; font-size: 1.1em; color: #059669; text-align: center;">3</span>
    <div><strong>Tease the promised land</strong> <span style="color: #777; font-size: 0.88em;">desirable, hard to reach alone</span></div>
  </div>
  <div style="display: grid; grid-template-columns: 2.2em 1fr; gap: 0.7em; align-items: center; padding: 0.5em 0.9em; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 4px solid #4f46e5; background: #eef2ff;">
    <span style="font-weight: 800; font-size: 1.1em; color: #4f46e5; text-align: center;">4</span>
    <div><strong>Features as magic gifts</strong> <span style="color: #777; font-size: 0.88em;">each one overcomes an obstacle</span></div>
  </div>
  <div style="display: grid; grid-template-columns: 2.2em 1fr; gap: 0.7em; align-items: center; padding: 0.5em 0.9em; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 4px solid #7c3aed; background: #f5f3ff;">
    <span style="font-weight: 800; font-size: 1.1em; color: #7c3aed; text-align: center;">5</span>
    <div><strong>Present evidence</strong> <span style="color: #777; font-size: 0.88em;">customer stories, metrics, demos</span></div>
  </div>
</div>

---

<!-- .slide: class="content-slide" -->
## Raskin element 1: change in the world

<p style="margin-top: 0.8em;">Raskin is about how you <em>open</em> with your problem: package it so the investor leans in instead of leaning out.</p>

<div class="grid-2" style="margin-top: 1.2em;">
<div class="card card-rose">
<h4>Static problem statement</h4>
<p style="font-size: 0.9em; font-style: italic;">"Hospitals lose medical equipment all the time."</p>
</div>
<div class="card card-emerald">
<h4>Same problem, reframed as change</h4>
<p style="font-size: 0.9em; font-style: italic;">"Hospital M&A has tripled in three years. Inventory systems weren't built for that."</p>
</div>
</div>

<p style="margin-top: 1.2em; color: #666; font-size: 0.88em;">The problem hasn't changed. The opening move has.</p>

---

<!-- .slide: class="content-slide" -->
## Raskin element 2: stakes

<p style="margin-top: 0.8em;">Once you've named the change, make the consequences real.</p>

<div class="card card-accent" style="margin-top: 1em;">
<p style="font-style: italic;">"Companies that move fast will capture this market. Companies that don't will be consolidated or displaced within 5 years."</p>
</div>

<p style="margin-top: 1em; color: #666;">The investor should be thinking: <em>which side of this do I want to be on?</em></p>

---

<!-- .slide: class="content-slide" -->
## Raskin element 3: the promised land

<p style="margin-top: 0.8em;"><strong>Before</strong> you show your solution, show the desired future state.</p>

<div class="callout callout-info" style="margin-top: 1em;">
<span class="callout-icon">💡</span>
<p><strong>Raskin's rule:</strong> the promised land must be <em>desirable and difficult for the prospect to reach without outside help</em>.</p>
</div>

<p style="margin-top: 1em; color: #666;">If the audience can imagine getting there on their own, you haven't described a compelling enough promised land.</p>

---

<!-- .slide: class="content-slide" -->
## Raskin element 4: features as magic gifts

<p style="margin-top: 0.8em;">Don't list features. Frame each one as a gift that overcomes a specific obstacle between here and the promised land.</p>

<div class="grid-2" style="margin-top: 1.2em;">
<div class="card card-rose">
<h4>Feature list</h4>
<p style="font-size: 0.85em; color: #666;">"AI-powered matching. Real-time alerts. Dashboard analytics."</p>
</div>
<div class="card card-emerald">
<h4>Gift framing</h4>
<p style="font-size: 0.85em; color: #666;">"Find who your team already trusts. No more cold applicants with fake LinkedIns."</p>
</div>
</div>

<p style="margin-top: 1em; color: #888; font-size: 0.82em;">This is essentially the same as saying focus on use cases, but it reminds you to weave a story and be eloquent about it.</p>

---

<!-- .slide: class="content-slide" -->
## Raskin element 5: evidence

<p style="margin-top: 0.8em;">You've promised a land. Now prove people can get there. Match your evidence to your claim.</p>

<div style="display: flex; flex-direction: column; gap: 0.35em; margin-top: 1em; font-size: 0.85em;">
  <div style="display: grid; grid-template-columns: 1fr 1fr; gap: 0.6em; align-items: center; padding: 0.4em 0.8em; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 4px solid #f97316; background: #fff7f0;">
    <span><strong>People want this</strong></span><span style="color: #555;">Signups, waitlist, letters of intent</span>
  </div>
  <div style="display: grid; grid-template-columns: 1fr 1fr; gap: 0.6em; align-items: center; padding: 0.4em 0.8em; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 4px solid #e11d48; background: #fff1f2;">
    <span><strong>People use this</strong></span><span style="color: #555;">DAU/MAU ratio, session frequency, retention at day 7/30</span>
  </div>
  <div style="display: grid; grid-template-columns: 1fr 1fr; gap: 0.6em; align-items: center; padding: 0.4em 0.8em; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 4px solid #059669; background: #f0fdf4;">
    <span><strong>People love this</strong></span><span style="color: #555;">A specific quote from a named person with context</span>
  </div>
  <div style="display: grid; grid-template-columns: 1fr 1fr; gap: 0.6em; align-items: center; padding: 0.4em 0.8em; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 4px solid #4f46e5; background: #eef2ff;">
    <span><strong>It works</strong></span><span style="color: #555;">Case study or white paper: named customer, baseline, outcome</span>
  </div>
  <div style="display: grid; grid-template-columns: 1fr 1fr; gap: 0.6em; align-items: center; padding: 0.4em 0.8em; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 4px solid #7c3aed; background: #f5f3ff;">
    <span><strong>The market is real</strong></span><span style="color: #555;">Named paying customer or signed pilot</span>
  </div>
  <div style="display: grid; grid-template-columns: 1fr 1fr; gap: 0.6em; align-items: center; padding: 0.4em 0.8em; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 4px solid #0891b2; background: #ecfeff;">
    <span><strong>You're growing</strong></span><span style="color: #555;">Week-over-week or month-over-month slope, not absolute numbers</span>
  </div>
</div>

---

<!-- .slide: class="content-slide" -->
## From narrative to deck

<p style="margin-top: 0.8em;">Your narrative is the argument. Your deck is the artifact that carries it.</p>

<div class="grid-2" style="margin-top: 1.2em;">
<div class="card card-indigo">
<h4>Narrative</h4>
<p style="font-size: 0.85em;">The story arc: why now, who's affected, what changes, why you. Lives in your head and your script.</p>
</div>
<div class="card card-accent">
<h4>Deck</h4>
<p style="font-size: 0.85em;">The visual sequence that carries the narrative. Slide by slide. The story tells you which slides you need and in what order.</p>
</div>
</div>

<p style="margin-top: 1.2em; color: #666; font-size: 0.88em;">Don't build the deck first. Build the narrative first, then let it dictate the structure.</p>

## Slide-by-slide: what goes on each

---

<!-- .slide: class="content-slide" -->
## Cover / Title

<p style="margin-top: 0.8em; font-size: 0.85em;"><strong>Job:</strong> Give the investor a mental model in 2 seconds.</p>

<div class="grid-2" style="margin-top: 1em;">
<div class="card card-emerald">
<h4>Include</h4>
<ul style="font-size: 0.85em;">
<li>Company name</li>
<li>One tagline: what you do, for whom</li>
<li>"The X for Y" framing</li>
</ul>
</div>
<div class="card card-rose">
<h4>Cut</h4>
<ul style="font-size: 0.85em;">
<li>Taglines like "the future of X"</li>
<li>Buzzword bingo</li>
<li>Your logo at 500px</li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>

<p style="margin-top: 1em; color: #666; font-size: 0.9em;">This is an opportunity to create an instant mental model. Don't miss it.</p>

---

<!-- .slide: class="content-slide" -->
## Slide 1: Problem

<p style="margin-top: 0.8em; font-size: 0.85em;"><strong>Job:</strong> Make the investor feel the pain. A great problem slide makes them nod; they recognize the reality you're describing. This is where you prove you've talked to real people.</p>

<div class="grid-2" style="margin-top: 1em;">
<div class="card card-emerald">
<h4>Include</h4>
<ul style="font-size: 0.85em;">
<li>A specific pain, for a specific person, in a specific context</li>
<li>Why current solutions <em>fall short</em>, not that they don't exist</li>
</ul>
</div>
<div class="card card-rose">
<h4>Cut</h4>
<ul style="font-size: 0.85em;">
<li>Abstract market conditions</li>
<li>Your company name (this slide is about the customer)</li>
<li>"Consumers demand better experiences" (everyone does)</li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>

<div class="callout callout-info" style="margin-top: 1em;"><span class="callout-icon">💡</span> The customer is the hero. You are the guide. Most first-time founders make themselves the protagonist. That's backwards. The transformation is in the customer, not in you.</div>

---

<!-- .slide: class="content-slide" -->
## Why Now

<p style="margin-top: 0.8em; color: #666;">The most skipped slide. Also the most powerful when done well.</p>

<div class="card card-accent" style="margin-top: 1em;">
<h4>Three to five timestamped external conditions</h4>
<ul style="font-size: 0.88em;">
<li>A declining cost curve</li>
<li>A regulatory change</li>
<li>A behavior shift</li>
<li>A new enabling technology</li>
<li>A consumer psychology shift</li>
</ul>
</div>

<p style="margin-top: 1em; color: #666; font-size: 0.88em;"><strong>Sequoia's sharp version:</strong> "Nature hates a vacuum. Why hasn't your solution been built before now?"</p>

---

<!-- .slide: class="content-slide" data-theme="rose" -->
## "Because AI" is not a Why Now

<p style="margin-top: 0.8em;">Vague AI reasoning is the 2026 version of "because the cloud."</p>

<div class="grid-2" style="margin-top: 1em;">
<div class="card card-rose">
<h4>Vague</h4>
<p style="font-size: 0.88em; font-style: italic;">"AI is transforming everything and we're riding that wave."</p>
</div>
<div class="card card-emerald">
<h4>Specific</h4>
<p style="font-size: 0.88em; font-style: italic;">"The cost of a single LLM inference dropped 85% in 18 months. A workflow that was $40 per user/month is now $2. Our business model didn't exist two years ago."</p>
</div>
</div>

---

<!-- .slide: class="content-slide" -->
## Slide 2: Solution

<p style="margin-top: 0.8em; font-size: 0.85em;"><strong>Job:</strong> Connect your mechanism directly to slide 1's pain. The investor should be able to draw a line from the problem you described to exactly what your product does about it. Investors spend about 33 seconds here. Clarity beats completeness.</p>

<div class="grid-2" style="margin-top: 1em;">
<div class="card card-emerald">
<h4>Include</h4>
<ul style="font-size: 0.85em;">
<li>What your product actually <em>does</em></li>
<li>The core mechanism</li>
<li>Why it's <strong>better</strong>, not just different</li>
</ul>
</div>
<div class="card card-rose">
<h4>Cut</h4>
<ul style="font-size: 0.85em;">
<li>Feature lists ("Intuitive. Powerful. Scalable.")</li>
<li>Technical architecture diagrams</li>
<li>Product roadmap</li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>

---

<!-- .slide: class="content-slide" -->
## Slide 3: Market

<p style="margin-top: 0.8em; font-size: 0.85em;"><strong>Job:</strong> Convince the investor the opportunity is big enough. Use <em>both</em> approaches: top-down for credibility and research context, bottom-up to show you've thought through the business model.</p>

<div class="grid-2" style="margin-top: 1em;">
<div class="card card-indigo">
<h4>Top-down</h4>
<p style="font-size: 0.85em; font-style: italic;">"The global recruiting market is $215 billion."</p>
<p style="font-size: 0.8em; color: #666; margin-top: 0.4em;">Establishes credibility. Shows you know the space. Gives investors a familiar frame of reference.</p>
</div>
<div class="card card-accent">
<h4>Bottom-up</h4>
<p style="font-size: 0.85em; font-style: italic;">"31,000 mid-size US companies × 12 reqs × $2,400/req/year = $890M addressable."</p>
<p style="font-size: 0.8em; color: #666; margin-top: 0.4em;">Shows business model thinking. Every number is defensible. Proves the math works.</p>
</div>
</div>

<p style="margin-top: 1em; color: #666; font-size: 0.85em;">Lead with top-down for scale, then ground it with bottom-up math.</p>

---

<!-- .slide: class="content-slide" -->
## Product

<p style="margin-top: 0.8em; font-size: 0.85em;"><strong>Job:</strong> Prove the thing exists and works. This is the "show don't tell" moment. One well-chosen screenshot should make your UX legible in 3 seconds; the investor should immediately see what the user sees and understand why it's valuable.</p>

<div class="grid-2" style="margin-top: 1em;">
<div class="card card-emerald">
<h4>Keep</h4>
<ul style="font-size: 0.85em;">
<li>One hero screen showing the core action</li>
<li>One sentence of caption</li>
<li>Annotations if the value isn't visually obvious</li>
</ul>
</div>
<div class="card card-rose">
<h4>Cut</h4>
<ul style="font-size: 0.85em;">
<li>A full product tour or six screenshots</li>
<li>Marketing copy ("Intuitive. Powerful.")</li>
<li>Anything requiring more than a 3-second glance</li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>

<p style="margin-top: 1em; color: #666; font-size: 0.85em;">Investors spend about 21 seconds here. Pick the screen that does the most work.</p>

---

<!-- .slide: class="content-slide" -->
## Slide 4: Business Model

<p style="margin-top: 0.8em; font-size: 0.85em;"><strong>Job:</strong> Prove the economics work. Investors need to understand the payment structure and unit economics before they can evaluate the investment: not just who uses the product, but who pays, what they pay for, and whether the margins are viable. DocSend data: investors spend <strong>94% more time</strong> on the Business Model slide in decks they go on to fund versus decks they pass on, even though the slide itself ranks 6th in absolute time. Investors who are seriously interested stress-test the economics harder.</p>

<div class="grid-2" style="margin-top: 1em;">
<div class="card card-emerald">
<h4>Answer these</h4>
<ul style="font-size: 0.85em;">
<li>Who <strong>pays</strong> (not who uses)?</li>
<li>What do they pay for, and how often?</li>
<li>Rough unit economics: LTV, CAC, payback period</li>
<li>Gross margins (this tells them your ceiling)</li>
</ul>
</div>
<div class="card card-rose">
<h4>Cut</h4>
<ul style="font-size: 0.85em;">
<li>5-year revenue projections (save those for Financials)</li>
<li>Vague pricing ("TBD" or "enterprise pricing")</li>
<li>"We'll figure out monetization at scale"</li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>

---

<!-- .slide: class="content-slide" -->
## Traction

<div class="grid-2" style="margin-top: 1em;">
<div class="card card-emerald">
<h4>If you have revenue</h4>
<ul style="font-size: 0.85em;">
<li>MRR / ARR</li>
<li>Growth rate MoM</li>
<li>Customer count</li>
<li>Retention</li>
</ul>
</div>
<div class="card card-emerald">
<h4>If you're pre-revenue</h4>
<ul style="font-size: 0.85em;">
<li>Active users, engagement</li>
<li>Waitlist depth</li>
<li>Signed pilots, LOIs</li>
<li>Notable partnerships</li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>

<div class="callout callout-info" style="margin-top: 1em;">
<span class="callout-icon">💡</span>
<p><strong>Show the slope, not the point.</strong> $50K MRR growing 25% MoM beats flat $500K MRR.</p>
</div>

---

<!-- .slide: class="content-slide" -->
## Slide 5: Competition

<p style="margin-top: 0.8em; font-size: 0.85em;"><strong>Job:</strong> Show you've mapped the landscape honestly and you have a defensible wedge. Investors see hundreds of decks and focus on specific industries; they know your competitors. Acknowledging them shows market sophistication; ignoring them signals naivety.</p>

<div class="grid-2" style="margin-top: 1em;">
<div class="card card-emerald">
<h4>Include</h4>
<ul style="font-size: 0.85em;">
<li>Direct competitors</li>
<li>Indirect alternatives</li>
<li><strong>The status quo</strong> (Excel, email, doing nothing)</li>
<li>Why you're different <em>and</em> defensible</li>
</ul>
</div>
<div class="card card-rose">
<h4>Cut</h4>
<ul style="font-size: 0.85em;">
<li>"No competition" (always wrong)</li>
<li>The 2×2 where you're alone in the upper-right</li>
<li>Dismissing incumbents without explaining their constraints</li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>

---

<!-- .slide: class="content-slide" data-theme="rose" -->
## "No competition" is never the right answer

<div class="grid-2" style="margin-top: 1em;">

## Slide 6: Team

<p style="margin-top: 0.8em; font-size: 0.85em;"><strong>Job:</strong> Answer "why these people, for this problem?" Investors are often backing the team as much as the idea. The single most important signal is specific domain experience that makes you uniquely positioned to solve this, not that you're smart or passionate.</p>

<div class="grid-2" style="margin-top: 1em;">
<div class="card card-emerald">
<h4>Include</h4>
<ul style="font-size: 0.85em;">
<li>Founders + key leadership</li>
<li>Photos, short titles</li>
<li><strong>"Why this person, for this problem"</strong></li>
<li>Specific domain experience that matters here</li>
</ul>
</div>
<div class="card card-rose">
<h4>Cut</h4>
<ul style="font-size: 0.85em;">
<li>Generic "passionate / experienced / driven"</li>
<li>Advisors standing in for founders</li>
<li>Companies you briefly worked at but didn't do the thing</li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>

<div class="callout callout-info" style="margin-top: 1em;">
<span class="callout-icon">💡</span>
<p>If your team is the <em>primary reason</em> to back this company, move this slide to <strong>position 2 or 3</strong>.</p>
</div>

## Slide 7: The Ask

<p style="margin-top: 0.8em; font-size: 0.85em;"><strong>Job:</strong> Make it easy for an interested investor to say yes. Be specific about what you need, how you'll deploy it, and what milestones it buys. Vagueness signals you haven't thought through the raise.</p>

<div class="grid-2" style="margin-top: 1em;">
<div class="card card-emerald">
<h4>Answer these</h4>
<ul style="font-size: 0.85em;">
<li>How much are you raising?</li>
<li>At what stage (pre-seed, seed)?</li>
<li>How will the capital deploy? (product, sales, hiring)</li>
<li>What milestones does this enable?</li>
<li>How much runway does it buy?</li>
</ul>
</div>
<div class="card card-rose">
<h4>Cut</h4>
<ul style="font-size: 0.85em;">
<li>Vague "we're looking for strategic partners"</li>
<li>No number at all</li>
<li>A use-of-funds pie chart with no milestones attached</li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>

<p style="margin-top: 1em; color: #666; font-size: 0.85em;">Plan for 24–36 months of runway. The old 18-month rule is obsolete.</p>

## Community-specific access

<ul style="margin-top: 1em; font-size: 0.92em;">
<li><strong>Your accelerator or startup lab:</strong> residency gives direct investor access; use it during the program, not just at demo day</li>
<li><strong>Alumni angel networks:</strong> people who've made money and want to back the next generation; searchable on LinkedIn</li>
<li><strong>Venture competitions:</strong> non-dilutive prize capital, plus visibility to judges who actively invest</li>
<li><strong>Student-run funds:</strong> Dorm Room Fund (First Round), Contrary, and similar funds operate at major universities and move fast</li>
<li><strong>Local seed ecosystem:</strong> every major city has 5–10 seed funds that specifically source from nearby communities</li>
</ul>

<p style="margin-top: 1em; color: #666; font-size: 0.85em;">Any shared affiliation, whether school, accelerator cohort, or industry community, turns a cold email into a semi-warm one. Use every connection you legitimately have.</p>

## What a real warm intro looks like

<p style="margin-top: 0.8em;">Not all introductions are equal. The value depends on who the introducer is and how well they know the investor.</p>

<div style="display: flex; flex-direction: column; gap: 0.45em; margin-top: 0.8em;">
  <div style="display: grid; grid-template-columns: 2.2em 1fr; gap: 0.7em; align-items: center; padding: 0.5em 0.9em; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 4px solid #059669; background: #f0fdf4;">
    <span style="font-weight: 800; font-size: 1.1em; color: #059669; text-align: center;">1</span>
    <div><strong>A founder the investor has already backed</strong> <span style="color: #777; font-size: 0.88em;">highest signal; same-week meeting</span></div>
  </div>
  <div style="display: grid; grid-template-columns: 2.2em 1fr; gap: 0.7em; align-items: center; padding: 0.5em 0.9em; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 4px solid #4f46e5; background: #eef2ff;">
    <span style="font-weight: 800; font-size: 1.1em; color: #4f46e5; text-align: center;">2</span>
    <div><strong>An angel already in your cap table</strong> <span style="color: #777; font-size: 0.88em;">co-investor vouching for you</span></div>
  </div>
  <div style="display: grid; grid-template-columns: 2.2em 1fr; gap: 0.7em; align-items: center; padding: 0.5em 0.9em; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 4px solid #f97316; background: #fff7f0;">
    <span style="font-weight: 800; font-size: 1.1em; color: #f97316; text-align: center;">3</span>
    <div><strong>A mutual professional contact</strong> <span style="color: #777; font-size: 0.88em;">moderate; depends on how well the contact is known</span></div>

    <span style="font-weight: 800; font-size: 1.1em; color: #e11d48; text-align: center;">4</span>

<p style="margin-top: 1em; color: #666; font-size: 0.85em;">The professional standard is the <strong>double opt-in:</strong> your contact confirms the investor wants to meet before making the connection. An intro the investor already agreed to is worth far more than a cold forward.</p>

<ul style="font-size: 0.85em;">
<li>Decide in 1–2 meetings; can wire in days</li>
<li>Betting on the <strong>founder</strong> more than the model</li>
<li>Check size: $10K–$100K typically</li>
<li>Want the story and credibility; less scrutiny on unit economics</li>
<li>Best ask: specific amount, no round mechanics required</li>
</ul>
</div>
<div class="card card-accent">
<h4>Seed funds</h4>
<ul style="font-size: 0.85em;">
<li>4–6 week process; partner meeting required</li>
<li>Betting on <strong>traction</strong> and path to Series A</li>
<li>Check size: $250K–$1M+</li>
<li>Will scrutinize unit economics, CAC, retention</li>
<li>Best ask: clear use of funds + 24-month milestones</li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>

<div class="callout callout-info" style="margin-top: 1em;">
<span class="callout-icon">💡</span>
<p>Build angel relationships first. Angels who write early checks become your most credible introducers to seed funds. That is the "angels to VCs" ladder.</p>
</div>

## Cold email: what actually works

<p style="margin-top: 0.8em;">Cold email is a last resort, not a strategy. But when you have no warm path, here is what raises the odds:</p>

<div class="grid-2" style="margin-top: 1em;">
<div class="card card-emerald">
<h4>What works</h4>
<ul style="font-size: 0.85em;">
<li>Lead with a specific metric or traction signal</li>
<li>One sentence on why <em>this investor</em>: cite their portfolio or writing</li>
<li>Under 150 words total</li>
<li>No deck attached: attach nothing</li>
<li>Subject line: specific and low-friction</li>
</ul>
</div>
<div class="card card-rose">
<h4>What kills response</h4>
<ul style="font-size: 0.85em;">
<li>"We're disrupting the $X billion market..."</li>
<li>No evidence you researched the firm</li>
<li>Deck attached to the first email</li>
<li>Long paragraphs explaining the business</li>
<li>Any phrasing implying mass distribution</li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>

<p style="margin-top: 1em; color: #666; font-size: 0.85em;">Realistic cold email response rate to VC partners: 1–3%. To angels and smaller funds: 5–10% if the email is genuinely well-crafted. The math favors finding warm paths.</p>
