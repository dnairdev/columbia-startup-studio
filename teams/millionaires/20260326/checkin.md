# Check-In: Post-Spring-Break

**Team:** Team Millionaires (DG-ST-AB)
**Date:** March 26, 2026
**Product:** QuestCity — gamified outdoor exploration app

---

## Look Back: Peer Review

### What worked when people used your product?

- The **core concept clicked immediately** — almost everyone got the "your city is an adventure game" framing without us explaining it. The quest example ("Find 3 hidden murals in Brooklyn") was the single most effective piece of copy; it made the abstract concept tangible.
- The **landing page converted well** in peer review. Multiple people said they would actually sign up, and two teammates from other groups did.
- The **achievement/progression system** resonated strongly with competitive users. The leaderboard framing generated the most energy in conversations — people lit up when they understood you could compete with friends.
- **Couple/group mode** was a clear differentiator. Reviewers called it out as something they hadn't seen before in exploration apps.

### What confused them or broke?

- **"Is this for tourists or locals?"** came up repeatedly. People assumed it was a travel app, not a hyperlocal one. Our positioning says "your neighborhood" but that wasn't landing fast enough.
- **No visual of the actual app.** Reviewers said the concept was compelling but they couldn't picture what they'd actually open on their phone. We have copy and a landing page — no mockup, no UI, nothing to demo.
- **Quest creation was unclear.** Several people asked: "Who makes the quests? You? The city? Me?" We never answered this on the landing page or in conversation.
- **Onboarding ambiguity.** How does a new user get from download → first completed quest? Nobody knew, including us. That flow doesn't exist yet.

### What's the biggest gap?

We have a compelling story but no product. The biggest gap is the distance between "here's a landing page" and "here's something you can actually use." Peer reviewers were interested in the idea but had no way to experience what we were promising. Without a working prototype — even a Wizard of Oz version — we're still selling a concept, not a product.

### One improvement we shipped based on feedback

Updated the landing page hero to answer "locals, not tourists" up front. Added a single line to the subheadline clarifying that QuestCity is for exploring **your own city**, not a new one. Also added an FAQ entry on quest creation: "Our team curates quests for each city. Later, power users can submit their own." Live at the existing Netlify URL.

## Look Forward: Your Roadmap

### What do we still need to learn about our product?

- **Who is actually our user?** Synthetic testing said couples 25–40, but peer review showed strong interest from solo explorers (20s, competitive, socially-motivated). We haven't talked to real waitlist signups yet — we need to run 5+ calls with people who actually signed up to understand who's actually excited.
- **Does onboarding work?** We don't know if a new user can get to their first completed quest without getting stuck or confused. The aha moment is "I just found a cool spot I never knew existed" — we have no idea if we can reliably deliver that.
- **Will people actually go outside?** Converting intent (sign-up) to behavior (leaving the couch) is the real challenge. Gamification can motivate, but the activation gap is enormous. We need to observe this with real users.

### What do we need to build between now and Demo Day?

1. **Working prototype (Wizard of Oz):** A version where users get real quests (we curate manually), check in via location or photo, and earn points tracked in a spreadsheet or simple backend. Enough to test the core loop without building the full system.
2. **Onboarding flow:** Sign up → set your city → get your first quest → complete it. This is the critical path. Build this first.
3. **Quest database (NYC, minimum 10 quests):** Real, tested quests in our backyard that we've personally walked. Quality over quantity.
4. **Basic progress tracking:** Users need to see their points, completed quests, and what's next. Even a simple screen accomplishes this.
5. **Analytics:** Track where users drop off in onboarding. This is our primary learning instrument post-launch.

### What's our team's biggest open question?

**Will the behavioral change actually happen?** Our entire thesis depends on people choosing QuestCity over Netflix on a Saturday afternoon. We've validated that the concept sounds appealing — we haven't validated that it changes behavior. This is the question that keeps us up at night and the one we most need to answer before Demo Day.

### What does success look like on May 12?

- **25+ users** have completed at least one quest (not just signed up — actually gone outside and done it)
- **3+ users** have completed 5 or more quests (showing retention, not just novelty)
- **One real quote** from a user describing a moment of genuine discovery they wouldn't have had otherwise
- A working prototype that a stranger could pick up and use without us explaining it
- Clear data on where users drop off so we can speak to what we'd fix next

If we have 25 people who actually went outside and found something new because of QuestCity, that's a real thing. Everything else is secondary.
