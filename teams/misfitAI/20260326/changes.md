# Peer Review Reflection

## What worked

During peer review, people understood that our app was related to fashion and wardrobe planning. They liked the overall idea of using technology to help organize clothing and generate outfits. The most promising part of the product was the vision-based feature, because it felt more unique and closer to the main purpose of the app.

## What confused people or broke

The biggest issue from peer review was that some people were confused about what the app actually did at first. In particular, the manual wardrobe flow made the product feel less clear, because peers were not sure whether the app was mainly for entering clothes by hand, searching for items, or using AI vision to identify clothing. We also heard that the wording around wardrobe inputs, especially "type" in the wardrobe section, was unclear. Another point of confusion was that "Search & Add" and "Manual" felt too similar, so users did not always understand why both options existed separately.

## Biggest gap

The biggest gap is clarity in onboarding and first impression. Our app needs to communicate its main value immediately: this is an AI wardrobe planner that can help users build a digital closet and generate outfits. Right now, that purpose is not obvious enough when users first interact with the wardrobe-adding flow.

## Improvement based on peer review

Based on the feedback, the most important improvement is to make the vision feature the primary first step and clarify the wording in the wardrobe flow. Leading with vision better shows what makes the app special, while clearer labels can reduce confusion about what users are supposed to enter. We would also simplify the experience by treating "Search & Add" and "Manual" as more closely related actions instead of presenting them as completely separate ideas.

## Reflection

This peer review was useful because it showed us that a strong idea is not enough if users do not understand the product right away. We learned that our interface needs to guide users more clearly and emphasize the feature that makes our app stand out. The feedback helped us see that our current design puts too much burden on the user to figure out the workflow for themselves.

Moving forward, we want to focus on clearer onboarding, better wording, and a more obvious starting point. If users immediately understand that the app can use vision to help build a digital wardrobe, they are more likely to see the value of the product. Overall, the peer review helped us identify that our biggest challenge is not the concept itself, but how clearly we present that concept to new users.

# Look Forward: Our Roadmap

## What do we still need to learn about our product?

We still need to learn who our real audience is and what use case matters most to them. Right now, our product could appeal to students, young professionals, or people interested in fashion organization, but we have not fully validated which audience benefits the most. We also need to learn whether onboarding feels smooth enough for first-time users and whether the "aha" moment happens early enough. Ideally, users should quickly understand that the app can scan or build their wardrobe and then turn that wardrobe into outfit recommendations. If that value is not obvious in the first interaction, then the product experience still needs work.

## What do we need to build between now and Demo Day?

Between now and Demo Day, we need to make the product feel clearer, more polished, and more reliable. Based on peer review and our open issues, the next priorities are:

- Make Vision the default wardrobe creation option so the most compelling feature is shown first.
- Refactor or combine the Manual and Search options so users are not confused by overlapping flows.
- Add dropdown options while building outfits so users have a more guided and consistent experience instead of relying on free-text inputs.
- Improve the Search API so it filters for fashion-related elements and avoids returning irrelevant results.
- Continue moving toward production by improving the backend and deployment setup, including cloud support for a more realistic demo-ready product.

## What is our team's biggest open question?

Our biggest open question is how to make the first-time user experience both simple and impressive. We know the app has value, but we are still figuring out the best way to introduce that value so users immediately understand what the product does and why it is useful. In other words, our main question is: how do we create an onboarding flow that is intuitive, highlights vision first, and leads users to the outfit recommendation experience as quickly as possible?

## What does success look like on May 12?

Success on May 12 means that a new user can open the app, understand the purpose right away, add clothing to their wardrobe without confusion, and generate outfit recommendations that feel relevant and polished. A successful Demo Day version should show a clear product story: users build a digital wardrobe, the app understands their clothing, and it helps them plan outfits in a way that feels useful and modern. From a technical perspective, success also means the app is stable enough to demo smoothly and the most confusing parts of the current MVP have been improved.

## Additional roadmap reflection

The roadmap shows that our biggest challenge is not just adding more features, but making the current features work together in a way that feels intentional. The issues we identified point to the same theme: clarity. Whether it is dropdowns instead of free text, better search filtering, or simplifying wardrobe creation, each improvement is really about reducing confusion and making the experience easier to trust. If we can tighten the onboarding, clarify the wardrobe-building flow, and present a smoother demo, we will be in a much stronger position by Demo Day.
